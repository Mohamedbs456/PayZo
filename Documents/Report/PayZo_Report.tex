\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}
\thispagestyle{plain}

\noindent\textbf{Abstract.}\quad
PayZo is a Tunisian digital-banking platform that unifies multi-bank account management and
protects peer-to-peer transfers through a machine-learning fraud detection system. The platform
is built on an N-tier architecture comprising a Spring Boot backend secured by Keycloak, a CBS
simulator, a three-layer ML orchestrator, and the client and staff applications. The client
side ships as a React web app and a React Native mobile app; the backoffice ships as an Electron
desktop application. The detection model pairs a calibrated LightGBM primary scorer with a
Random Forest fallback, trained on 500,000 synthetic transactions generated from per-user
behavioural profiles, and reaches a PR-AUC of 0.91 on the test set. High-risk transactions are
suspended and routed to an analyst review queue, so a human decides on every fraud alert.

\vspace{6pt}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} digital banking, fraud detection, machine learning, peer-to-peer
transfer, Tunisia.

\chapter*{General Introduction}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{General Introduction}
\markboth{General Introduction}{}

Tunisia's electronic payment landscape is changing fast. The Banque Centrale de Tunisie
reports 27.9 billion dinars processed through digital channels in 2024 alone\webcite{web:bct1}{1}, and mobile
payments grew 81\% in 2025\webcite{web:bct2}{2}. At the centre of that growth is peer-to-peer transfer: instant,
convenient, and irreversible. The last property is also its biggest vulnerability. Once a
fraudulent transfer executes, the funds are gone. Reversal is not a realistic option in a
live banking environment.

Fraud has kept pace with digitisation. Attackers now run automated pipelines and use
generative AI tools to scale attacks that previously required human effort. The global cost of
fraud exceeds \$400 billion annually\webcite{web:veriff}{3}, and AI-assisted fraud losses are projected to reach \$40
billion by 2027\webcite{web:deloitte}{5}. Manual review cannot scale to meet that threat. A platform that wants to stay
trusted has to answer automation with automation.

PayZo was designed around that constraint. It is a Tunisian digital-banking platform that
addresses two problems at once. The first is fragmentation: most clients hold accounts across
multiple banks, but no single surface gives them a complete view of their funds. PayZo resolves
this by connecting to a Core Banking System that aggregates account data across institutions.
The second problem is fraud: every peer-to-peer transfer passes through a machine-learning
scoring engine before the money moves, and transactions flagged as high risk are suspended
pending analyst review. The two sides reinforce each other: a unified account view means
the model sees the full picture of a client's activity, not just one bank's slice of it.

The project was carried out at Proxym-IT, a Tunisian software company based in Sousse that
builds digital products for the banking and financial sector. Its flagship platform, Bankerise,
provided the professional context and the technical environment for the work.

The development followed the 2TUP (Two-Track Unified Process) methodology. Two parallel
tracks, one functional and one technical, converged into a single realisation phase. The
functional track defined the actors, use cases, and interaction flows. The technical track
determined the architecture, the technology choices, and the infrastructure. Both tracks had
to advance together on a 14-week, single-developer schedule, and 2TUP's Y-shape fits that
constraint: it forces the design work to happen before the build work, without separating the
two so far that they drift.

This report documents the project from first principles to deployed system. Chapter 1 frames
the problem and the solution. Chapter 2 defines the actors and their requirements. Chapter 3
covers the architecture and the design. Chapter 4 builds the fraud-detection model step by
step, from the training data to the final three-layer orchestrator. Chapter 5 presents the
working system, its development environment, the implementation across its client and staff
interfaces, and the test results.

\section{Problematic}

Cash is losing ground in Tunisia. Electronic transactions hit 27.9 billion dinars in 2024
(+10.6\%), and mobile payments rose 81\% in 2025 according to the Banque Centrale de
Tunisie\webcite{web:bct1}{1}\webcite{web:bct2}{2}. About half of that is peer-to-peer
transfer. The mode is taking off because it is instant and irreversible; the same two
properties also make it a target.

The global picture puts the stakes in perspective. Fraud losses exceed \$400 billion
annually (Veriff\,/\,TechRadar)\webcite{web:veriff}{3}. Deepfake fraud incidents rose over
3,000\% in a single year (VentureBeat\,/\,Deloitte)\webcite{web:deepfake}{4}. Losses driven
by generative AI tools stood at \$12.3 billion in 2023 and are projected to reach \$40
billion by 2027, according to the Deloitte Center for Financial
Services\webcite{web:deloitte}{5}.

Fraud has industrialised in step with that growth. Attackers run automated pipelines with
AI assistance; one script can hit thousands of accounts at once. Manual review cannot keep
that pace, and the math is one-sided: defenders have to be right every time, an attacker
only needs to win once. If trust slips, users fall back to cash and the whole digitalisation
effort gets wasted. We now look at what existing platforms offer against this backdrop.

\section{Proposed solution}

A problem like this has to be attacked at the root first, then on the active side. Otherwise
the defender is always one step behind.

PayZo's root fix removes the fragmentation. The platform sits on top of a Core Banking
System (CBS) and brings a person's accounts across banks into a single layer, so the system
has a complete picture of all the client's activity, so fraud cannot slip into any of the blind
spots between banks.

The active layer answers automation with automation. A machine-learning service scores
every peer-to-peer transfer before the money moves, and flagged cases go to a human analyst
to take the final decision.

Every operation is built to feel easy and direct. Whether on the client side or the backoffice,
the experience must remain simple and frictionless. Figure~\ref{fig:payzo-logo} shows the
PayZo brand identity.

\subsection{Functional requirements}

PayZo's features divide naturally along role lines. The requirements below capture what each
actor can do within their scope.

\vspace{4pt}\noindent\textbf{Client}\par\nopagebreak
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item \textbf{Registration.} The client submits their CIN; the system previews the matching
        CBS record; after OTP verification the account waits for admin approval.
  \item \textbf{Authentication.} Log in with credentials and confirm via an OTP channel; first
        login triggers a forced password change.
  \item \textbf{Account overview.} View all linked accounts across banks with their current
        balance.
  \item \textbf{Transaction history.} Browse the paginated transaction history for each account.
  \item \textbf{Peer transfer.} Initiate a transfer via a saved beneficiary, @username, or
        manual RIB and name; confirm with OTP; the ML service scores the transaction before
        it executes.
  \item \textbf{Own-account transfer.} Move funds between the client's own accounts across
        banks; no OTP or ML scoring required.
  \item \textbf{Beneficiary management.} Save, rename, mark as favourite, and delete recipients
        keyed by RIB.
  \item \textbf{Pre-transfer assist.} Resolve a RIB, verify a recipient's name, or look up a
        PayZo username before submitting a transfer.
  \item \textbf{Profile management.} Edit profile picture, edit @username, set a default account, and reset the
        password from within the profile.
  \item \textbf{Notifications.} Receive in-app and email notifications for key account events.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{4pt}\noindent\textbf{Admin}\par\nopagebreak
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em]
  \item \textbf{Client registration.} Approve or reject pending client registrations, or

  \item \textbf{Client management.} View client profile and transaction history; block or
        unblock access.
\end{itemize}

\section{System architecture}

PayZo follows a layered N-tier architecture, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:sysarch}.

The presentation layer holds the client mobile app and the backoffice desktop application.
Both authenticate through Keycloak, which runs two separate realms: one for clients, one for
backoffice staff. The application layer is a single Spring Boot service whose logic is split
across five modules: identity and access, transaction processing, bank synchronisation,
notification delivery, and fraud scoring. The data layer holds all persistent state in
PostgreSQL; the CBS runs as a separate data source for the bank catalogue and account
information. Outbound calls go to the ML service for fraud scoring and to external email and
SMS providers for notifications. At the edge, an Nginx reverse proxy fronts the stack on
port~80, routing client traffic to the frontend and API calls to the Spring Boot backend.\\

The same figure also previews the concrete deployment of the platform. The technology behind
each component is detailed in Chapter~5 (Table~\ref{tab:tech}), and the runtime environment is

\section{Feature engineering}

The model scores a transfer on two levels: how it compares to the general population, and how
it compares to this specific sender's own history. The second level is the design choice the
datasets in the previous section could not support.

Twenty-four features, listed in Table~\ref{tab:features}, are grouped into four categories.

Three of the four groups (transaction, velocity, and trust \& relationship) sit at the
population level; the fourth, per-user norm, is computed against the sender's own history.

Fraud is relative to the sender. A large overnight transfer is routine for some users and
suspicious for others. The per-user norm features encode each transaction against the sender's
own 30-day history rather than against a population threshold.

The payoff is measurable. An earlier iteration of the model, built before this per-user layer
existed, plateaued at a validation PR-AUC of about 0.67: with only population-level signals it
could not separate a transfer that is out of character for a given sender from one that is
merely large. Introducing the per-user behavioural profiles and the eight per-user-norm
features raised validation PR-AUC to about 0.87 --- the single largest
gain in the project, and the empirical justification for the per-user design.

\section{Algorithm selection}

We ran five candidates under the same protocol, then let the numbers decide; Table~\ref{tab:benchmark} reports their validation PR-AUC.

The data was split by time: months 0--8 for training (\textasciitilde375K transactions),
month 9 for validation (\textasciitilde42K), months 10--11 for testing
(\textasciitilde83K).

Class imbalance was handled through model weighting, not oversampling. We tested SMOTE~[15] and
dropped it. On heterogeneous tabular data it generates synthetic minority rows that bear little
resemblance to real fraud, which inflated training scores without improving production
behaviour.

PR-AUC was the selection metric. On a 1.5\% fraud rate, ROC-AUC is optimistic, because the
negative class dominates the denominator. Precision-Recall does not have that problem~[14].

\section{Final model}

The two selected algorithms sit inside a three-layer structure, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:mlpyramid}.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{MLPyramid.png}
\caption{Three-layer scoring orchestrator}
\label{fig:mlpyramid}
\end{figure}

\noindent\textbf{Layer 1} is LightGBM with isotonic calibration~[7]. It is the main scorer and runs on every
transfer, turning the transaction's features into a fraud probability between 0 and 1. It was
the strongest of the five candidates tested, which is why it carries the everyday scoring. The
calibration step is what makes that probability trustworthy: it lines the model's output up
with the real fraud rate.

\noindent\textbf{Layer 2} is Random Forest, also isotonic-calibrated. As a bagging model with balanced class
weights, it reaches its decision differently from Layer 1's gradient boosting. Layer 2 takes over when Layer 1 is down or unavailable, so the platform never loses the ability to score a transfer.

\noindent\textbf{Layer 3} is not a machine learning model. It is a rule-based algorithm with fixed logic and no
learned parameters. It does not depend on any trained artefact and is always available. Five
rules cover the patterns the ML layers find hardest: a large transfer to an unknown recipient,
a velocity burst, a high-amount night transfer, a large transfer from a new account, and a
savings drain after hours.\\

Every score maps to one of three risk levels: LOW, MEDIUM, or HIGH. LOW transfers execute
immediately. MEDIUM and HIGH transfers are suspended and routed to the analyst review queue. Table~\ref{tab:test-metrics} reports the test-set metrics for the two learned layers.

Fraud detection cannot afford gaps. If both learned layers are down, Layer 3 still fires. There
is always a decision.

\section{Conclusion}

This chapter built the fraud detection system step by step. We started from the gap left by
existing datasets, defined 24 features around per-user behavioural norms, generated the training
data to carry them, and benchmarked five algorithms before settling on the three-layer
orchestrator that runs in production. The per-user norm features are what separate this model
from a static rule set. The always-available structure is what makes it reliable enough to sit
on the critical path of every transfer. Chapter 5 presents the full implementation.

\subsection{Integration tests}

Integration tests verify the full request path from API call to database. Security is validated
at every entry point: JWT tokens are checked against the Keycloak JWK endpoint on each request,
the \texttt{BlockedUserFilter} returns 403 for blocked accounts, OTP verification is
rate-limited to three attempts before invalidation, and every RIB input passes a mod-97 check
before any CBS call is made. In a banking platform where transfers are instant and irreversible,
these checks are not optional.

\section{Deployment overview}
\label{sec:deployment}

The full system architecture was presented in Figure~\ref{fig:sysarch} (Chapter~3). At
runtime, the platform's services are packaged as containers and orchestrated through a
single Docker Compose stack: the Spring Boot backend, the CBS simulator, Keycloak, PostgreSQL,
the ML inference server, the web applications, and an Nginx reverse proxy all start from
one command. Each service is reachable on its own
port within the Compose network, and the whole stack runs end to end on a single development
machine. On startup, the CBS simulator populates its in-memory ledger with a realistic seed of
50 clients, roughly 110 accounts, and 1,600 transactions. Figure~\ref{fig:docker-desktop} shows
the running stack in Docker Desktop.

\section{Conclusion}

This chapter presented the working system across its client and staff interfaces. The development environment ran entirely on a single machine, with the full Docker stack in parallel. The mobile app and the Electron backoffice were the primary delivery surfaces, covering the client and staff sides respectively. Tests validated the main application flows and confirmed that every security gate on the transfer pipeline holds. The next section closes the report with a summary of what was achieved and the directions left open for future work.

%==============================================================
%  GENERAL CONCLUSION & PERSPECTIVES
%==============================================================
\chapter*{General Conclusion and Perspectives}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{General Conclusion and Perspectives}
\markboth{General Conclusion and Perspectives}{}

This project set out to answer a specific problem: Tunisian digital banking is growing fast,
peer-to-peer transfers are its most vulnerable surface, and no existing platform combines
unified account management with inline fraud scoring in a single system. PayZo is that system.

The backend is a Spring Boot service secured by Keycloak, connected to PostgreSQL and a CBS
simulator, and deployable as a single Docker Compose stack. The fraud-detection layer trains
on 500,000 synthetic transactions generated from per-user behavioural archetypes; the final
model is a three-layer orchestrator with LightGBM as the primary scorer (PR-AUC 0.91,
ROC-AUC 0.99) and a rule-based firewall as the last resort. Transactions flagged at medium or
high risk go to an analyst review queue. The client uses a React web app and a React Native
mobile app; staff use a React web app and an Electron desktop app with role-scoped views for the Admin,
Analyst, and SuperAdmin roles.

\subsection*{Limitations}

The training data is synthetic. Real transaction history from the BCT or a partner institution
is a prerequisite for any live deployment, and that validation has not been done. The platform
is also Tunisia-specific and handles only Tunisian dinars; multi-currency or cross-border
transfers would require regulatory review and data-model changes outside the current scope.

\subsection*{Perspectives}

Replacing the synthetic dataset with real, anonymised BCT data is the most direct path to a
better model, as it would allow threshold calibration against actual fraud rates. On the
product side, biometric authentication on the transfer confirmation step would close the gap
most OTP-bypass attacks exploit. Further out, a proper deployment pipeline with automated
testing and a staging environment tied to a real CBS is what converts this prototype into
something a banking partner could run in production.
